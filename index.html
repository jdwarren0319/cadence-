Rhetorical questions
A rhetorical question is one you ask without expecting an answer. It pulls the audience into your point of view by making them mentally agree before you say anything.
Example: Carlin — "Have you ever noticed that anybody driving slower than you is an idiot, and anyone going faster than you is a maniac?" He never answers it. The audience answers it themselves, and in doing so they agree with him.

Rule of threes
When you list three things, the first two set up a pattern and the third breaks it. That break is the laugh. Two items feel incomplete. Four items dilute the punch because you’ve already broken the pattern and kept going.
Example: "I need three things to be happy — good coffee, good music, and someone else to blame." The first two are reasonable. The third breaks the pattern. That’s the joke. If there were four items, the fourth would land flat because the surprise already happened on three.

Anaphora
Anaphora means starting multiple sentences with the same word or phrase. It works like a drumroll — each repetition builds tension, and the audience leans forward waiting for the pattern to break. When it finally does, the release is bigger because of the buildup.
Example: Carlin — "The planet is fine. The people are fucked." He could have just said "people are fine and the planet is fine" but the repetition of "the _____ is fine" creates a rhythm that makes the break land harder. Your script uses "not for a car, not a bike, not for food" — same principle, no conjunction, just repetition and rhythm.

Reversals
A reversal is when the second half of a sentence contradicts or undercuts the first half. You set up an expectation, then flip it. The bigger the gap between what they expected and what you said, the bigger the laugh. The key word is usually "but," "however," "except," or "turns out."
Example: "She was the kindest person I ever met — but she made me genuinely afraid." The first half points you toward warmth. The second half lands somewhere completely different. Your Sombr script does this: "Sombr has 500 times the money I have — but have you seen his Spotify picture?" Success sets up the reversal to absurdity.

Pattern interrupts
YouTube data shows that viewers drift every 2–3 minutes if the energy stays the same. A pattern interrupt is anything that breaks the rhythm and re-engages attention — a change of pace, a physical bit, a hard pivot, a sudden short sentence after many long ones, or a question directed at the audience. You need to script these deliberately, not hope they happen.
Example: MrBeast documents this obsessively — "crazy progression" means never letting 3 minutes pass at the same energy. For your show, the needle drop IS a pattern interrupt. So is a sudden close-up, cutting to a different camera angle, or a single short sentence like "Named sombr." after a long setup. The app flags comedy gaps — stretches with no punchline — as potential interrupt zones.
Write more to detect pattern interrupt opportunities.
Weasel words
Weasel words are phrases that hedge, soften, or qualify your statement. They kill conviction. Comedy requires certainty — even if what you’re certain about is absurd. When you say "kind of" or "sort of" or "basically," you’re signalling to the audience that you don’t fully believe what you’re saying. They feel that and it weakens the joke.
Example: "I kind of think he might be spreading his butt cheeks" lands nothing. "Is he spreading his butt cheeks?" lands the joke. The certainty of the question form is funnier than the hedged statement. Cut the weasels or replace them with commitment.

Passive voice
Active voice puts the subject first and the action second: "I wanted money." Passive voice buries the action: "Money was something I needed." Active voice hits harder because it’s direct — the subject does the verb, there’s no distance between the person and the action. Passive voice sounds evasive, which works against comedy’s need for conviction.
Example: Active — "Sombr dropped out of school." Passive — "School was something Sombr was caused to leave." The active version is faster, cleaner, and funnier because it doesn’t hedge. Carlin almost never used passive voice.

Vague language
Specific details are funnier than general ones. This is because specificity signals truth — the audience trusts you more when you know exactly what you’re talking about. And the specific detail itself is often funnier than any joke you could add around it.
Example: "a car" is information. "A 1974 Buick Riviera with a broken AC and a pine tree air freshener that smells like regret" is a joke. "Some music" tells us nothing. "The Doors’ Greatest Hits" tells us everything about who this person is and why they want it. The specificity IS the joke. Flag: "thing," "stuff," "someone," "somewhere," "people," "various."
